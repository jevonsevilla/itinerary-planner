# Kansai Region, Japan — Travel Research

_Researched for: osaka-2026 | Travel dates: 2026-04-17 to 2026-04-24 | Budget: mid-range_

## Quick Facts

**Country:** Japan
**Language:** Japanese (fewer than 30% speak English at any level, less than 8% fluently)
**Currency:** Japanese Yen (¥)
**Time Zone:** Japan Standard Time (JST, UTC+9)
**Electricity:** 100V, 50/60 Hz (50 Hz in Eastern Japan, 60 Hz in Western Japan including Osaka/Kyoto). Plug types A and B (same as US/Canada). Most two-pronged US devices work without an adapter, but three-pronged plugs may need a Type B to Type A adapter.
**Tipping:** No tipping culture in Japan. Tipping is not expected and may even be refused, as great service is built into the price. The philosophy is rooted in "omotenashi" (wholehearted service done with pride, not for reward).

## Weather & When to Visit

**Mid-to-Late April Conditions:**
During your travel dates (April 17-24), expect pleasant spring weather with daytime temperatures around 19°C (66°F) and nights around 11°C (52°F). The weather is mostly sunny and dry with low possibility of rain, and any rainfall typically comes fast and goes quickly.

**Cherry Blossom Season:**
Peak cherry blossom season in Osaka, Kyoto, and Nara occurs around early April (full bloom around April 1-3). By mid-to-late April, peak bloom will have passed in major cities, though some blossoms may still linger. Higher altitude areas like Mount Yoshino could still offer full blooms during this period, with blossoms peaking near the top in mid-to-late April. The second half of April tends to be uncrowded and lovely, making it an excellent time to visit if you prefer fewer crowds.

**What to Pack:**
- Light layers for variable temperatures (warm days, cool nights)
- Light jacket or sweater for evenings
- Comfortable walking shoes
- Umbrella or light rain jacket (just in case)
- Respectful clothing for temple/shrine visits (covered shoulders and knees recommended)

## Entry Requirements

**Visa Requirements for Filipino Passport Holders:**
Filipino passport holders **need a visa** to visit Japan for tourist purposes, as the Philippines is not included in Japan's visa waiver program. Key points:

- **Visa Fee:** Waived for Philippine nationals, but applicants must pay a PHP 520 service fee to VFS Global (JVAC)
- **Processing Time:** At least 5 working days from submission; during peak seasons like cherry blossom (March-April), processing may take several weeks
- **Application Centers:** Available in Manila, Cebu, and Davao
- **e-Visa:** Electronic Visa issuance started on December 15, 2025, though applications must still be submitted through the Visa Center
- **Duration:** Short-term Stay Visa allows up to 90 days for tourism throughout Japan

**Passport Validity:** Ensure your passport is valid for the duration of your stay.

**Vaccination Requirements:** No specific vaccination requirements for entry from the Philippines (check current health advisories before departure).

## Safety & Health

**General Safety:**
Japan is one of the safest countries in the world for solo travelers. Violent crime is very rare, and tourist areas are usually well-lit. It's very safe for solo travelers to walk alone, even at night in major cities.

**Emergency Numbers:**
- **Police:** 110
- **Fire/Ambulance:** 119
- **Japan Helpline:** 0570-000-911 (24-hour English assistance for emergencies, lost items, travel problems)
- **JNTO Japan Visitor Hotline:** 050-3816-2787 (24/7 in English, Chinese, Korean, Japanese)

**Solo Traveler Tips:**
- Women-only train carriages available during peak commute times
- Solo dining is very normal in Japan
- Head to a local koban (small police boxes throughout neighborhoods) if you need help
- Download Safety Tips app for J-Alerts in English and emergency language cards

**Health & Hygiene:**
- **Tap Water:** Completely safe to drink throughout Japan; meets some of the highest standards in the world
- **Food Safety:** Excellent food hygiene standards; food poisoning is rare
- **Pharmacies:** Widely available in cities
- **Travel Insurance:** Strongly recommended, as many domestic health plans don't cover international travel
- **Masks:** While no longer required, some Japanese still wear masks as a courtesy

**Natural Disaster Awareness:**
Japan is prone to natural disasters such as typhoons, earthquakes, tsunamis, and volcanic eruptions. Download emergency apps like NHK World and Safety Tips for alerts.

**Areas to Avoid:**
Generally very safe throughout, but exercise normal caution in entertainment districts late at night.

## Getting Around

**JR Pass:**
The nationwide Japan Rail Pass pays off only for long-distance travel and is **not worth purchasing for travel around the Kansai Region alone**. The pass does not cover non-JR transportation (subways, buses) which is often most convenient in Osaka and Kyoto.

**ICOCA Card:**
**Important 2026 Update:** ICOCA card sales are being discontinued sequentially starting from items that run out of stock after November 2025, with facility benefits no longer offered after March 2027.

**KANSAI ONE PASS:**
Prepaid IC cards accepted on major trains and buses in the Kansai region, including transportation facilities with ICOCA, PiTaPa, and IC markings. These function as rechargeable payment cards for trains, buses, and even convenience stores.

**Transportation Within Osaka:**
- **Subway:** Midosuji Line runs north-south connecting Umeda, Shinsaibashi, Namba
- **Osaka Loop Line:** JR line circling central Osaka
- **Bus:** Extensive network but subway is usually easier for tourists
- **Walking:** Namba to Shinsaibashi is walkable; Dotonbori area very pedestrian-friendly

**Inter-City Transport (Osaka ↔ Kyoto ↔ Nara ↔ Kobe):**

**Osaka to Kyoto:**
- JR trains: 30-50 minutes, frequent service
- Private railway lines also available

**Osaka to Nara:**
- JR Yamatoji Line from JR Namba or Tennoji: 30-45 minutes to JR Nara Station
- Best for: Deer Park and Todaiji Temple (10-minute walk from station)

**Osaka to Kobe:**
- JR Tokaido-Sanyo Line (special rapid) from Osaka Station to Sannomiya: 20-30 minutes, ¥420
- Very frequent service throughout the day

**Kobe 1-Day Loop Bus Ticket:** Available in 1-day or 2-day options for unlimited rides on CITY LOOP Bus and PORT Loop routes.

**Walkability:**
Osaka's Namba, Dotonbori, and Shinsaibashi areas are very walkable. Kyoto's Gion, Higashiyama, and Arashiyama areas are best explored on foot. Nara Park and surrounding temples are walkable from the station.

## Where to Stay

**Best Neighborhoods in Osaka:**

### 1. Namba/Minami Area (Top Choice for First-Time Visitors)
**Pros:**
- Heart of tourist attractions (Dotonbori, Shinsaibashi)
- Excellent food scene and nightlife
- Namba Station is a major transport hub (Midosuji subway, Nankai trains)
- Ideal for day trips to Kyoto, Nara, Kobe
- Lots to see and do within walking distance

**Cons:**
- Can be crowded and noisy, especially near Dotonbori
- Tourist-heavy area

**Best For:** Solo travelers who want convenience and vibrant atmosphere

### 2. Umeda/Kita Area
**Pros:**
- Osaka's most important transport hub (4 train lines converge)
- Huge shopping, dining, and entertainment district
- Clean, wide, safe sidewalks
- Close to Osaka Station (JR lines, Haruka Express to/from airport)
- Museums, Umeda Sky Building, Kids Plaza Osaka nearby

**Cons:**
- More business-oriented; less "tourist atmosphere" than Namba
- Slightly farther from Dotonbori

**Best For:** Travelers prioritizing transport connections and modern shopping

### 3. Honmachi Area
**Pros:**
- More residential and business-oriented (quieter, low-key)
- Between Namba and Umeda on Midosuji Line
- Short subway ride to Osaka Castle, Umeda, or Dotonbori
- Local restaurants and cafes

**Cons:**
- Fewer attractions in immediate vicinity
- Less nightlife

**Best For:** Travelers seeking a quieter base with easy access to main areas

**Accommodation Types:**

**Business/Mid-Range Hotels:**
Cost between ¥20,000 (US$136) and ¥50,000 (US$341) per night. Osaka offers solid mid-range hotels and even luxury stays for less than what you'd pay in Tokyo or Kyoto. The Leben is highly recommended for reasonable prices and convenient location. Average mid-range hotel: ¥18,171 (US$118) per night.

**Capsule Hotels:**
Budget-friendly option (from ¥5,000/night) offering small self-contained sleeping pods with shared facilities. Usually no minimum stay requirement. Good options:
- Millennials Kyoto (modern, co-living concept with lounges/co-working)
- Neco Republic's Cat Ryokan in Osaka (cat-themed, can be booked by one group)
- Sauna & Capsule Hotel Rumor Plaza in Kyoto (male-only, with saunas and onsen)

**Ryokan (Traditional Inns):**
Budget ryokan from ¥5,000/night (hostel-like, shared facilities). Mid-range: ¥15,000-¥30,000/night. Luxury: Over ¥50,000/night. Rooms typically designed for 2-4 occupants; more occupants = reduced per-person rates.

**Hostel/Guesthouse:**
Budget option for solo travelers, often under ¥5,000/night in dorms.

## Food & Drink

**Must-Try Osaka Dishes:**

**Takoyaki (Octopus Balls):**
Osaka's most iconic street food. Battered balls filled with octopus, topped with takoyaki sauce, mayo, bonito flakes, and seaweed. Cost: ¥300-¥800 per serving.
- **Abeno Takoyaki Yamachan** (Tennoji/Abeno districts): One of the most famous and highly-rated
- **TAKOPA (Takoyaki Park)** at Universal City Walk: Sample 5 of Osaka's best takoyaki shops under one roof

**Okonomiyaki (Savory Pancake):**
Osaka-style okonomiyaki originated here. Grilled batter with cabbage, meat/seafood, topped with sauce, mayo, bonito flakes. Cost: ¥800-¥1,500.
- **Mizuno** (Dotonbori): Michelin-recommended, famous for yam flour okonomiyaki and "Mizuno-yaki"
- **Chibo** (Dotombori Arcade): Popular and English-friendly
- **Ajinoya** (near Dotombori): Bib Gourmand specialist with fast, friendly service
- **Jibundoki**: Known for "Buta-tama" (pork and egg) okonomiyaki

**Kushikatsu:**
Deep-fried skewers of meat, seafood, and vegetables. Important rule: Don't double-dip in the communal sauce!

**Kushi-age:**
Similar to kushikatsu, found throughout Osaka's izakayas.

**Regional Kyoto Cuisine:**
- Kaiseki (multi-course traditional meal)
- Yudofu (tofu hot pot)
- Matcha (green tea) desserts

**Nara Specialties:**
- Kakinoha-zushi (sushi wrapped in persimmon leaves)
- Narazuke (pickled vegetables)

**Kobe:**
- **Kobe Beef:** World-famous marbled beef. For budget travelers, look for lunch deals at restaurants offering cheaper Kobe beef lunch sets rather than dinner pricing.

**Dining Tips for Solo Travelers:**
- Solo dining is very normal and common in Japan
- Ramen shops, sushi counters, and standing bars are solo-friendly
- Many restaurants have counter seating perfect for solo diners
- Don't eat or drink while walking (considered rude)
- Wait for the highest-ranking person to start eating/drinking in group settings
- Say "itadakimasu" before eating, "gochisousama deshita" after finishing

**Food Safety:**
- Tap water is completely safe to drink
- Excellent hygiene standards throughout Japan
- Raw fish is safe to eat
- Convenience store food is high quality

**Hidden Dining Districts:**
- **Ura Namba:** Hidden dining district between Doguyasuji Shotengai and Namba Station, filled with local favorites offering Okinawan, Chinese, and other cuisines
- **Tenma:** Alleys near JR Tenma Station fill with locals after work; great for casual drinks and quick bites
- **Hozenji Yokocho:** Charming 80-meter cobblestone alley near Dotonbori with ~50 traditional restaurants

## Top Attractions

### Osaka

**Osaka Castle**
- **Description:** Iconic symbol of Osaka, originally built in 1583, reconstructed in 1931
- **Hours:** 9:00-17:00 (Nov-Feb until 16:30); extended to 21:00 during cherry blossom season

- **Access:** Tanimachi Yonchome Station (15-min walk)

**Dotonbori**
- **Description:** Osaka's most vibrant entertainment district with giant neon signs, street food, shopping, nightlife
- **Hours:** 24/7, but most lively in evening/night
- **Admission:** Free (individual food/shopping costs vary)
- **Must-See:** Glico Running Man sign, canal views, street food
- **Time Needed:** 2-3 hours (or more for dining/nightlife)

**Shinsaibashi Shopping Arcade**
- **Description:** Covered shopping street stretching 600m with hundreds of shops
- **Hours:** Most shops 10:00-20:00
- **Admission:** Free
- **Time Needed:** 1-2 hours

**Sumiyoshi Taisha Shrine**
- **Description:** One of Japan's oldest Shinto shrines, founded in 211 AD
- **Hours:** 6:00-17:00
- **Admission:** Free
- **Time Needed:** 1 hour

**Osaka Aquarium Kaiyukan**
- **Description:** One of the world's largest aquariums
- **Hours:** 10:00-20:00 (varies)

- **Time Needed:** 2-3 hours

**Universal Studios Japan**
- **Description:** Theme park with Nintendo World, Harry Potter areas
- **Hours:** Varies by season
- **Admission:** From US$54.52 for 1-day pass, US$103.34 for 2-day pass (prices vary by date; book early). Note: Tickets NOT sold on-site; must book from authorized partners (Klook, Rakuten, Traveloka, Headout)

**Abeno Harukas**

**Kuromon Ichiba Market**

### Kyoto

**Fushimi Inari Shrine**
- **Description:** Famous for thousands of vermilion torii gates forming trails up the mountain
- **Hours:** Open 24/7
- **Admission:** Free
- **Time Needed:** 1-3 hours (depends how far up mountain you hike)
- **Access:** JR Nara Line to Inari Station (5-min from Kyoto Station)

**Kinkakuji (Golden Pavilion)**
- **Description:** Stunning Zen temple covered in gold leaf
- **Hours:** 9:00-17:00
- **Admission:** ¥500 adults, ¥300 students
- **Time Needed:** 45 min - 1 hour

**Arashiyama Bamboo Grove**
- **Description:** Magical bamboo forest path
- **Hours:** Open 24/7 (best early morning before 8:30 AM or late evening to avoid crowds; peak crowding 10:00 AM-3:00 PM)
- **Admission:** Free
- **Time Needed:** 30 minutes - 1 hour
- **Night Visits:** Magical on full moons, but bring flashlight as lighting is limited

**Kiyomizu-dera Temple**
- **Description:** Historic temple with famous wooden stage and city views
- **Hours:** 6:00-18:00 (extended during special seasons)
- **Admission:** ¥400
- **Time Needed:** 1-2 hours

**Gion District**
- **Description:** Historic geisha district with traditional wooden machiya houses
- **Hours:** 24/7 for streets; shops/restaurants vary
- **Admission:** Free to walk around
- **Time Needed:** 1-2 hours
- **Hidden Gem:** Shimbashi street, considered "most beautiful street in Asia," especially during spring with cherry blossoms and azaleas

**Philosopher's Path**
- **Description:** Canal-side walking path (especially beautiful during cherry blossom season)
- **Hours:** 24/7
- **Admission:** Free
- **Time Needed:** 30-45 minutes

### Nara

**Nara Park & Deer**
- **Description:** Vast 660-hectare park with over 1,000 free-roaming sacred deer
- **Hours:** Open 24/7
- **Admission:** Free (deer crackers for feeding sold throughout park)
- **Time Needed:** 2-3 hours (can combine with Todaiji)
- **Access:** 10-minute walk from JR Nara Station or Kintetsu Nara Station

**Todaiji Temple**
- **Description:** Houses 15-meter-high Great Buddha statue; one of Japan's seven great temples
- **Hours:** 8:00-17:00 (varies by season)
- **Admission:** ¥600
- **Time Needed:** 1 hour
- **Location:** Within Nara Park

**Kasuga Taisha Shrine**
- **Description:** Shinto shrine famous for hundreds of bronze and stone lanterns
- **Hours:** 6:00-18:00
- **Admission:** Inner area ¥500
- **Time Needed:** 1 hour

### Kobe

**Kobe Harborland & Meriken Park**
- **Description:** Waterfront area with shopping, dining, Kobe Port Tower (108m tall for city views)
- **Hours:** 24/7 for park; tower and shops vary
- **Admission:** Park free; tower admission fee applies
- **Time Needed:** 2-3 hours

**Nankinmachi (Kobe Chinatown)**
- **Description:** One of Japan's three major Chinatowns with 100+ restaurants and shops
- **Hours:** Most shops/restaurants 10:00-20:00
- **Admission:** Free
- **Must-Try:** Steamed buns (manju), ramen, tapioca drinks
- **Time Needed:** 1-2 hours
- **Access:** 5-min walk from Motomachi Station, 10-min from Sannomiya Station

**Kitano Ijinkan (Foreign Residences)**
- **Description:** Historic district with former merchants' mansions-turned-museums
- **Hours:** 9:00-18:00 (varies)
- **Admission:** Individual homes charge ¥300-¥700
- **Time Needed:** 2-3 hours

**Kobe Nunobiki Herb Gardens**
- **Description:** Japan's largest herb gardens with 75,000 herbs and flowers; ropeway offers spectacular views of Kobe and Osaka Bay
- **Hours:** 10:00-17:00 (varies by season)
- **Admission:** Ropeway + Gardens combo ticket
- **Time Needed:** 2-3 hours

## Day Trips

### Osaka to Kyoto
**Distance:** 30-50 km
**Transport:** JR trains (30-50 minutes, frequent service) or private railway lines
**Cost:** Varies by line; ~¥560-¥800 one-way
**What to See:** Temples (Kinkakuji, Fushimi Inari, Kiyomizu-dera), Arashiyama Bamboo Grove, Gion District, Philosopher's Path
**Time Needed:** Full day or half-day depending on itinerary
**Tips:** Start early to beat crowds at popular temples; use Kyoto buses or rent a bicycle for local transport

### Osaka to Nara
**Distance:** ~40 km
**Transport:** JR Yamatoji Line from JR Namba or Tennoji Station (30-45 minutes to JR Nara Station)
**Cost:** Affordable with IC card
**What to See:** Nara Park (free-roaming deer), Todaiji Temple (Great Buddha), Kasuga Taisha Shrine
**Time Needed:** Half-day (can see deer park and Todaiji in 3-4 hours) or full day
**Tips:** Buy deer crackers (¥200) to feed the deer; walk from station to park takes ~10 minutes; deer can be pushy when they smell food!

### Osaka to Kobe
**Distance:** ~30 km
**Transport:** JR Tokaido-Sanyo Line (special rapid) from Osaka Station to Sannomiya (20-30 minutes, ¥420)
**Cost:** ¥420 one-way
**What to See:** Nankinmachi (Chinatown), Kobe beef restaurants, Kitano Ijinkan, Kobe Harborland, Meriken Park, Kobe Port Tower, Nunobiki Herb Gardens
**Time Needed:** Half-day to full day
**Tips:** Kobe 1-Day Loop Bus Ticket for unlimited rides on CITY LOOP and PORT Loop buses; look for Kobe beef lunch deals rather than dinner pricing for budget savings

### Multi-City Combination
**Osaka → Nara → Kyoto (or reverse):**
Possible in one long day using JR lines, but will be rushed. Better to dedicate separate days to each city.

## Useful Phrases

**Basic Greetings:**
- **Hello:** Konnichiwa (こんにちは) - kohn-nee-chee-wah
- **Good morning:** Ohayou gozaimasu (おはようございます) - oh-hah-yoh goh-zah-ee-mahs
- **Good evening:** Konbanwa (こんばんは) - kohn-bahn-wah

**Courtesies:**
- **Thank you:** Arigatou gozaimasu (ありがとうございます) - ah-ree-gah-toh goh-zah-ee-mahs
- **Excuse me/I'm sorry:** Sumimasen (すみません) - soo-mee-mah-sen (very versatile: can mean excuse me, I'm sorry, or used to get attention/express gratitude)
- **Please:** Onegaishimasu (お願いします) - oh-neh-gah-ee-shee-mahs
- **You're welcome:** Dou itashimashite (どういたしまして) - doh ee-tah-shee-mah-shee-teh

**Dining:**
- **Before eating:** Itadakimasu (いただきます) - ee-tah-dah-kee-mahs
- **After eating:** Gochisousama deshita (ごちそうさまでした) - goh-chee-soh-sah-mah desh-tah
- **Delicious:** Oishii (おいしい) - oh-ee-shee
- **Check please:** Okaikei onegaishimasu (お会計お願いします)

**Directions & Travel:**
- **Where is...?** ...wa doko desu ka? (...はどこですか？)
- **Train station:** Eki (駅) - eh-kee
- **Toilet:** Toire (トイレ) - toh-ee-reh
- **Exit:** Deguchi (出口) - deh-goo-chee
- **Entrance:** Iriguchi (入口) - ee-ree-goo-chee

**Shopping:**
- **How much?** Ikura desu ka? (いくらですか？) - ee-koo-rah des kah
- **Too expensive:** Takai (高い) - tah-kah-ee

**Emergency:**
- **Help:** Tasukete (助けて) - tah-soo-keh-teh
- **I don't understand:** Wakarimasen (わかりません) - wah-kah-ree-mah-sen
- **Do you speak English?** Eigo o hanasemasu ka? (英語を話せますか？)

**Cultural Note:** Bowing while saying thank you adds emphasis to gratitude. A deeper, longer bow shows more respect.

## Cultural Etiquette

**Temple & Shrine Etiquette:**

**Shrine vs. Temple:**
- **Shrine:** Shinto structure (look for torii gates)
- **Temple:** Buddhist structure

**Entering a Shrine:**
- Bow in front of the torii gate at entry
- Walk on the sides of the path (middle is for gods)
- At purification fountain: Take ladle → rinse left hand → rinse right hand → pour water into cupped hand → rinse mouth (spit beside fountain, not back in) → rinse ladle handle
- At worship hall: Put money in offering box → ring bell → bow deeply twice → clap hands twice → pray with hands together → bow deeply once

**Entering a Temple:**
- Put coins in offering box before main worship hall
- Quietly put hands together in front of chest and pray
- **Do NOT clap** when paying respects at a temple (clapping is for shrines only)
- No purification ritual at temples

**General Respect:**
- Keep volume low; temples and shrines are places of worship
- Dress respectfully (covered shoulders and knees recommended)
- Photography usually allowed but no flash, and never inside main halls unless permitted
- Remove shoes when entering buildings (look for entrance step/shoe racks)

**Dining Etiquette:**
- Don't stick chopsticks upright in rice (funeral ritual)
- Don't pass food chopstick-to-chopstick (funeral ritual)
- Don't rub wooden chopsticks together (implies restaurant is cheap)
- Slurping noodles is acceptable and even encouraged
- Say "itadakimasu" before eating, "gochisousama deshita" after
- Don't walk while eating or drinking (bad manners)
- At izakaya/group settings, pour drinks for others; someone will pour for you

**General Behavior:**
- Remove shoes when entering homes, ryokan, some restaurants/temples
- Be quiet on trains; no phone calls
- Stand on left side of escalators (Osaka), walk on right (opposite of Tokyo)
- Queuing: Wait in orderly lines; don't push
- Blowing nose in public is considered rude; sniffling is more acceptable
- Tattoos may restrict entry to some onsen (hot springs) and public baths
- Don't tip (see Tipping section)

**Public Transport:**
- Stand in orderly lines
- Let passengers exit before boarding
- Keep phone on silent; no phone calls
- Women-only carriages available during peak times

**Gift-Giving:**
- If giving a gift, use both hands
- Gifts are often not opened in front of giver

**Respect Personal Space:**
- Japanese culture values personal space
- Physical contact (hugs, handshakes) is less common; bowing is standard greeting
- Apologize for bumping into someone ("sumimasen")

## Connectivity

**Internet Options:**

**eSIM (Recommended for 2026):**
Trending option because it's built into your phone as a permanent embedded chip; no need to replace physical SIM card. When you buy and install an eSIM, you give your phone instructions to connect to local Japanese networks.

**Popular eSIM Providers:**
- **Airalo:** Connects to SoftBank; consistently reliable, intuitive app, instant top-ups. Good for short trips and month-long stays.
- **Truely:** Most reliable for remote areas; provides unlimited data across SoftBank, AU, and NTT Docomo networks.
- **ESIMJAPAN.com:** Best plans for travelers to Japan; roams to KDDI au network with average download speeds of 487Mbps.
- **Sakura Mobile:** Operates under NTT docomo and au networks.

**Pricing:** High-speed data from $13.99 for 20GB, $29.99 for 50GB, or $70 for 1TB.

**Physical SIM Cards:**
Available at airports and electronics stores, but less convenient than eSIM.

**Pocket WiFi:**
Shareable internet device you can rent; good for groups. Can be picked up at airport and returned before departure.

**Free Wi-Fi:**
Available at major stations, airports, cafés, but not available throughout the city. Relying solely on free Wi-Fi is risky; best to also have SIM/eSIM/pocket WiFi.

**Useful Apps:**

**Navigation & Transport:**
- **Google Maps:** Surprisingly accurate for Japan train itineraries and times; includes platform info and which train car to ride for easy transfers. Even locals use it.
